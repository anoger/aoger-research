Impact of Interactivity

Exploration of the impact of interactivity degree (dialogue vs. video) and thematic alignment of the agent on student interest. In the interactive condition, students could freely dialogue with an embodied historical character powered by an LLM (Large Language Model). In the video condition, students watched pre-recorded YouTube videos of the same character.

Interactivity seems to enhance interest

Positive effect of direct interaction on student interest, confirmed across different grade levels

The effect of thematic alignment appears complex

Influence modulated by cognitive development: different impact across grade levels (7th, 9th grade, high school)

Design and Metacognitive Biases

Societal Challenge: The advent of LLMs and video generators now enables the creation of virtual agents with striking photorealism, capable of delivering fluid and convincing discourse. This technological convergence raises major questions about the metacognitive biases they may induce.

This study examines how the agent's appearance (humanoid vs. abstract) influences the illusion of understanding in students.

Experimental software: Interface specifically developed for the study on illusion of understanding in middle school students.

About Me

PhD student in Human-Computer Interaction at Arts et Métiers (LAMPA - Presence and Innovation Team), I specialize in studying LLM-based virtual agents in real educational contexts.

My research combines technical architecture (facial animation, LLMs) and experimental evaluations in school settings to understand how agent interactivity and design influence student engagement, interest, and critical perception.

Particularly interested in evaluating and mitigating the illusion of understanding induced by these technologies, I also apply my knowledge in generative AI through teaching activities and educational event organization.

Research Approach

My work is distinguished by an ecological approach: rather than limiting myself to laboratories, I conduct my studies directly in classrooms, with real teachers and students. This approach enables essential field validation for the actual adoption of these technologies.
